Overview

Tout ce qui ne tue pas rend plus fort

The front lever is a skill that challenges gravity by balancing the body in a horizontal position, parallel to the ground while holding to a bar.

The front lever is considered by many as one of the hardest skills in calisthenics, however, it is rated A in gymnastics (A being the easiest level of difficulty). Indeed, the front lever is somewhat an easy skill to achieve if you follow a specific, well-constructed plan.

This skill combines three important factors that helps the athlete hold the front lever in a straight line.

I-Core strenght

Tout ce qui ne tue pas rend plus fort

The body of the athlete is in a shape of a lever, it means two straight unbent lines crossing at a pivot point.

Line 1 is the line joining the hand holding to the bar and the shoulders of the athlete: the alignment of the different joints is maintained by the triceps and the forearm muscles; straightening the arm is quiet an easy task.

Line 2 is the line going from the shoulders to the very end of the toe’s phalanges. The alignment of the joints is maintained by a huge amount of muscles working together to fulfill the task. All the vertebras have to be placed at a specific position to align the back. The pelvis has to be placed and rotated to a specific degree to hold the legs is a horizontal position. The inferior limbs must stay in a straight line to balance the body. Foot position is not very important due to the fact that an average foot weights 400 grams.

The alignment if the vertebras and the rotation of the pelvis are set by the CORE, the muscle group that joins the thoracic cage and the pelvis and that controls the body position in almost every skill in the sport. Having a string core is a must in the front lever.

II-Pulling strenght

Tout ce qui ne tue pas rend plus fort

The pulling strength you get from calisthenics is insane, the force you can generate from your back and arms muscles is incredible and can exceed 100 kg very easily.

The trick with the front lever is the position of the elbow, it must be in a straight unbent position wish requires a good triceps strength on order to execute the extension of the joint. This position puts more stress on the pulling muscles in the back as all the weight is now lifted by the extensors of the superior limbs.

Straight arms strength is an important component of many skills in calisthenics like the planche and the front lever. This strength can be improved overtime through straight arms exercise. If you unlock this component, you can easily jump to the next level with bent arms front lever skills like the touch front lever or front lever pullups.

Motion is a human body feature that combines different components to move a body part.

It all starts with the decision making in the respective cortex of the brain, then it travels through the motor cortex (4 & 6) to go through a specific nerve for each muscle. The process of making the decision to execute a skill and to transfer it to the muscle is very complex, and it can be improved in many ways in order to improve the execution of the skill.

The decision of doing the skill in itself is complex, and it needs a pre-elaborated pattern of muscles recruitment to position the body in the horizontal position. This pattern is improved overtime through repetition and consistent effort and will to improve.

The transfer of the information is improved overtime through the reconstruction of synaptic connections to transfer the message very quickly and effetely without information loss.

The contraction of the muscle is improved with various strengthening exercises.

This process helps overtime, build a string muscle memory that is hard to lose even after years of no practice. You never forget how to ride a bike.

Requirements

You can start training for the front lever with zero experience is the sport, however, we recommend some requirements that would help you get started:

  • Discipline
  • A consistent number of pullups (~10): pull strength.
  • A consistent number of dips: straight arms strength.

Progressions

The front lever progression are set in a way that would enable the athlete to increase the difficulty through the angle of the legs. The more the legs are bent, the easier it gets.
One can achieve the skill by mastering the following progressions:

  • I. Tuck front lever
  • II. Advanced Tuck front lever
  • III. Advanced Tuck plus
  • IV. Full front lever

I. Tuck Front lever

This progression will help you familiarize yourself with the straight arms pulling strength required for the actual skill, it emphasizes on the horizontality of the body and the positioning of the scapula.

The main goal of this progression is to comprehend the interaction between your arms, shoulders and the rest of your body. This interaction would result in a perfect flexion angle of the shoulders to keep the body parallel to the ground.

Once you hold your first 3 seconds of the tuck front lever, try to find a suitable positioning for your scapula, a position that is comfortable, and that gives the best leverage to move your shoulders. Also, try to keep your body as horizontal as possible.

The optimal position of the scapula is a semi-retracted position that is not exaggerated and as close to neutral as possible. The scapula plays a major role in the esthetic look of the front lever and many other skills. Learn more about it in this article about the Scapula.

II. Advanced Tuck Front lever

This progression doesn’t look much different from the previous one, but it is a game changer when it comes to the full front lever and even more advanced variations of the skill. It might look the same, but the strength you will acquire from this progression is incredible.

The main difference would be the hips flexion angle that must be equal to 90°, this will place your knees in front of the bar and will keep the belly area clear for some advanced movements.

This progression will work on your straight arms pulling strength, but also the core strength as we start to unbend our legs. This is a crucial progression due to the fact that your body has to find the optimal pelvis position to keep your body straight. The simplest way to do so is to make sure your pelvis is as horizontal as possible, and your belly forms a straight line instead of a curve.

III. Advanced Tuck plus

This progression is just a difficult variation of the last one, and is the transition to the full front lever. Same goal, higher difficulty; All you have to do is to advance your feet and get closer to the full front lever position.

IV. Full Front lever

The perfect position of the front lever is the position where we can see a straight line joining the heels, the knees, the hips, and the shoulders.

The pelvis plays a major role in holding the form together, and its rotation affects the esthetic look of the skill. If you want the perfect form possible do this:

Tilt the pelvis backwards and push it forward. A backwards tilt and a forward thrust are a key component in the perfect form. Learn more about it in this article about the Pelvis.

What's Next ?

The front lever is only the start to the pull skills world. This skill will not only open up new doors for you, but also unlock an insane pulling strength that will radiate to other skills.
If you are comfortable with this skill, you might want to consider getting to the next level, and try to achieve one of the following skills:




Touch front lever hold

This skill is one of the best skills to learn after mastering the straight arms hold. You might think that it is easier with the bent arms, but you will soon be surprised by the real challenge: keeping contact with the bar, or as we call it in the park: “THE TOUCH”. Learn more about this incredible skill in this article.

Front lever pull-ups

This skill is the actual next level of mastering the pull strength. Keeping the body straight and horizontal wile pulling up to the bar until “THE TOUCH” occurs. This skill is a reps based skill that puts you in a face-to-face fight with gravity. Learn more about it in this article.

Victorian front lever

This skill is a must for every athlete who want to execute some power combos. It is a front lever held on the forearms, on top of parallel bars. Basically, a front lever touch, with no touch. The goal is the hold as high as possible and not collapse under pressure.

It holds two variations:

  • 1. Victorian on parallel bars: the hardest and the most challenging

  • 2. Victorian on single bar: easier variation, the goal is to touch the bar with your stomach, still easier than the front lever touch hold.
